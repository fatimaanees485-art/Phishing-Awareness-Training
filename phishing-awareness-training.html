How To Recognize Phishing Emails

Phishing messages try to make a risky action feel normal, urgent, or required. Slow down when an email asks you to click, open, pay, sign in, approve, or share sensitive information.

?

Sender mismatch

The display name looks familiar, but the actual address uses a misspelling, odd domain, free email account, or unrelated company.

!

Pressure or fear

The message claims your account is locked, an invoice is overdue, a package failed, or a manager needs something kept secret.

$

Money or data request

Be cautious with gift cards, wire transfers, payroll changes, tax forms, passwords, MFA codes, or confidential files.

#

Link deception

The text says one thing, but hovering over it reveals a different destination, URL shortener, or look-alike domain.

A

Unusual wording

Awkward grammar is one clue, but modern attacks can be well written. Trust behavior signals more than spelling alone.

*

Unexpected attachment

Invoices, shared documents, scans, voicemail files, and password-protected archives are common delivery methods for malware.

How To Spot Fake Websites

A fake site may look polished and still be dangerous. Check the address, the context, and the action being requested before entering credentials or payment details.

Website safety checks

  • Type the address yourself or use a trusted bookmark for banking, payroll, cloud storage, and government services.
  • Read the domain from right to left: in login.company.com, the true domain is company.com.
  • Do not trust a lock icon alone. HTTPS protects the connection, but scammers can also use HTTPS.
  • Watch for misspellings, extra words, unusual top-level domains, and urgent pop-ups that demand immediate action.
  • Never enter an MFA code on a page opened from a suspicious link.

Social Engineering Tactics Attackers Use

Attackers target attention and emotion. Their goal is to make you act before you verify.

Common pressure levers

  • Authority: Pretending to be an executive, IT admin, bank, government agency, or law enforcement contact.
  • Urgency: Creating a deadline that discourages normal review, such as "today only" or "account disabled in 30 minutes."
  • Scarcity or reward: Offering refunds, coupons, bonuses, crypto returns, or limited access.
  • Trust chaining: Using a compromised vendor, customer, or coworker mailbox to make the request feel legitimate.
  • Secrecy: Asking you not to tell anyone because the matter is confidential, sensitive, or a surprise.

The safer response pattern

  • Pause before clicking, approving, paying, or sharing information.
  • Verify through a second channel you already trust, such as a known phone number or saved website.
  • Report suspicious messages instead of forwarding them to coworkers.
  • Use a password manager so fake domains do not auto-fill credentials.
  • Turn on multi-factor authentication, preferably with an authenticator app or security key.
  • Keep devices and browsers updated so malicious links have fewer ways to exploit them.

Real-World Examples

These patterns are based on public consumer and law-enforcement guidance. The names below are training scenarios, but the tactics are common in actual incidents.

Account hold scam

Scenario: A message claims a streaming, payment, or bank account is on hold because billing information failed.

Red flags: Generic greeting, link to update payment, pressure to avoid suspension.

Best move: Open the service from your saved app or bookmark, not from the email.

Business email compromise

Scenario: A vendor or executive asks for a payment change, wire transfer, or gift cards.

Red flags: Secrecy, unusual timing, new bank details, or "I am in a meeting" excuses.

Best move: Confirm through an existing phone number and follow approval procedures.

Fake government portal

Scenario: A search result, text, or email sends you to a look-alike reporting, refund, tax, or benefits site.

Red flags: Non-.gov domain, requests for bank details, misspelled agency name, or payment demand.

Best move: Navigate directly to the known official site and report suspicious pages.

Key Takeaways

Phishing defense is a habit: pause, inspect, verify, report.

Before you click

  • Look at the real sender address and destination URL.
  • Be skeptical of urgent account, payment, password, or prize messages.
  • Open sensitive services from known apps, bookmarks, or typed addresses.

If something went wrong

  • Change affected passwords from the legitimate site.
  • Report the message to your organization, email provider, or the FTC.
  • Run security updates and scans if you opened an attachment or downloaded a file.
